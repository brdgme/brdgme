# Texas Hold'em - Advanced Strategy

Higher-level strategic considerations.

## Hand strength across streets

- Your hand's value changes as community cards arrive. A pair that is best on the flop can be crushed by a straight or flush that completes on the turn or river - reassess after each card.
- Draws (four to a flush or straight) have value even when you are currently behind, because they can become the best hand. Weigh the cost of calling against how often the draw completes.
- The best possible hand on a given board (the "nuts") is worth betting and raising hard; a merely good hand is worth less when the board is scary.

## Position

- Acting later is an advantage: you see what opponents do before you decide. Play more hands and more aggressively from late position, tighter from early position.
- The dealer acts last on every post-flop round, so the dealer seat is the most profitable position at the table.

## Pot odds and implied odds

- Compare the cost to call against the current pot. If the pot offers more than the odds of completing your draw, a call is profitable even if you usually miss.
- Implied odds matter: a cheap call now can win a large pot later if you hit and an opponent keeps betting. Conversely, if you and your opponent are nearly all in, there is little extra to win.

## Reading opponents and betting patterns

- A player who raises into several opponents usually has a strong hand. A player who only calls is often on a draw or holding a medium hand.
- Sudden aggression from a previously passive player often signals a made hand; treat their raises with respect.
- Track stack sizes. A short-stacked player is likely to shove all in soon, so calling stations and bluffs change value against them.

## Tournament survival

- The game ends when one player holds all the money, so survival has value. Early on, avoid risking your whole stack on coin-flip situations.
- As the minimum bet doubles every 5 hands, blinds grow and force action. Short stacks must eventually commit; pick a hand and a moment rather than bleeding chips to the blinds.
- When you have a big stack, pressure shorter stacks who cannot afford to call without a real hand. When you are short, look for a spot to double up rather than fade away.

## Bluffing

- A bluff only works if it can make a better hand fold. Bluffing a player who will call with anything wastes chips.
- Bluffs are more believable when they match the story of the board and your earlier betting. A raise on a card that completes a plausible flush or straight is far more convincing than a random bet.
- Do not bluff multiple opponents at once; the more players in the hand, the more likely someone has a real hand and calls.
