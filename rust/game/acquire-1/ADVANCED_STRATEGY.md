# Acquire Advanced Strategy

## Majority positioning
- A lead of 4 shares is generally unassailable because opponents can only buy 3 per turn. Once you have a 4-share lead, stop buying and diversify.
- Owning 13 shares guarantees absolute majority in any corporation (25 total shares). Beyond that, additional shares only increase sell value, not bonus share.
- Contest majority only when the bonus payout exceeds the cost of buying in. A major bonus on a size-3 corporation is barely worth the investment.
- In the endgame, majority in a large corporation about to be merged is worth more than majority in a safe corporation that will only pay at game end.

## Merger timing
- Use tile placement to delay mergers you are not ready for. If you need one more turn to buy shares in the target corporation, play a tile elsewhere.
- Force mergers when you hold majority in the acquired corporation. The bonus is paid immediately and the 2-for-1 trade gives you a position in the survivor for free.
- When multiple mergers are possible from a single tile, the order matters. Bonuses are paid largest-acquired first, and each player resolves shares before the next merger.
- Growing a corporation to size 11 makes it safe. If you hold majority in a small corporation threatened by a larger neighbour, racing to 11 protects your investment.

## Cash management
- Keep enough cash to buy 3 shares in the cheapest active corporation at all times. This preserves your ability to respond to new foundations.
- Cash is king in the endgame. At game end all shares are sold at current price, so over-investing in illiquid positions is fine only if the game is truly ending.
- After receiving a merger bonus, resist the urge to immediately reinvest everything. Wait to see what the next player does before committing.
- In 2-player games, cash management is tighter because the dummy player dilutes your bonus income. Budget for receiving only half the expected bonus.

## Reading opponents
- Watch opponents' share counts in the player table. If an opponent is accumulating shares in a small corporation, they are positioning for its merger bonus.
- If an opponent has no shares in a corporation you plan to merge, the merger costs them nothing - they will not resist it.
- Track which corporations have few bank shares remaining. If only 1-2 shares are left, opponents cannot buy in and your majority is locked.
- An opponent who just received a large bonus is dangerous - they have the cash to contest your positions. Expect aggressive buying next turn.

## Endgame
- When the tile bag is low, stop growing corporations and start consolidating share positions. The game can end on any player's turn.
- Trigger the end when your total liquidation value (cash + all shares at current price + expected bonuses) exceeds every opponent's.
- If you are behind, do not trigger the end. Instead, force mergers where you hold the acquired majority to swing cash in your favour.
- In the final round, buying shares is still valuable because they are sold at current price at game end. Buy into the largest corporations for maximum liquidation value.

## 2-player special rule
- The dummy player rolls 1-5 shares in every merger, competing for your bonuses. Assume you will lose roughly half of each bonus payout.
- To guarantee a bonus in 2-player, you need more shares than the dummy's maximum roll of 5. Holding 6+ shares in the acquired corporation secures majority over the dummy.
- The dummy never trades or sells, so its shares are removed from competition permanently after each merger. This makes later mergers slightly more profitable for you.
- With only one real opponent, majority contests are binary. Either you outspend them or you do not - there is no third party to split the bonus with.
