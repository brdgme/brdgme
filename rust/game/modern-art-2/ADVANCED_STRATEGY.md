# Modern Art - Advanced Strategy

Higher-level strategic considerations.

## Artist value is cumulative

- An artist's value is the sum of what it earned in every round so far. An artist that took 1st ($30) in round 1 is worth $30 per card in every later round too, even if it places nowhere later.
- Cards you hold pay out at the artist's total value at the end of the round you hold them. A card of a high-value artist is worth buying even at a steep price.
- Pushing an artist you hold heavily into the top three raises the value of every card you own of that artist.

## Controlling the round end

- The round ends the moment a 5th card of any artist is played. Hold the 5th card of a popular artist as a lever: play it to end the round when you are cashed up and ahead, hold it to keep buying when you need more cards.
- The 5th card earns its player nothing. Treat playing it as a tempo move, not a scoring move.
- Count how many of each artist are on the table (the public purchases) so you know how close each artist is to ending the round.

## Auctioneer economics

- As auctioneer, a buyer's payment comes to you; paying for your own card goes to the bank. Selling is profit, buying your own is a cost. Structure auctions to sell.
- Fixed Price lets you name the price. Set it high enough to extract value if someone buys, but low enough that you are content to keep the card yourself if nobody does.
- Open auctions let you ride competing bidders up to a good sale price before the hammer falls.

## Reading opponents

- Watch each opponent's purchases and cash. A player loaded with one artist will bid hard to protect its value; a player near $0 cannot compete.
- In Open auctions you can drive an opponent's price up and then pass, forcing them to overpay - but only if you are sure you will not be left holding the winning bid.
- Deny opponents cheap cards of an artist they are collecting; bid or buy to make them pay full value.

## Auction-type tactics

- Sealed auctions are won outright by the highest single bid. Bid close to the most the card is worth to you, since you cannot react to others.
- Once Around rewards later position: later bidders see earlier bids and can shade just above them. The auctioneer only bids if nobody else does.
- Double auctions sell two cards for one price. Adding a card doubles the lot's value to the winner, so bids tend to be larger - good for you as the seller.

## Money across rounds

- Cash carries between rounds. Spending everything in round 1 leaves you unable to buy in later rounds, when artist values are usually higher.
- Artist values only grow, so preserving cash for rounds 3 and 4 is often worth more than buying aggressively early.
- In the final round there is no future to save for - spend what you can convert into cards that pay out this round.
