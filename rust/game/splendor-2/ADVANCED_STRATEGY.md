# Splendor - Advanced Strategy

Higher-level strategic considerations.

## Building the engine

- Early cards are investments, not points. Most level 1 cards are worth 0 prestige but their bonuses make every later card cheaper, so prioritise bonus colours that line up with the cards you want next.
- Pick one or two bonus colours to specialise in and stack them. A focused engine buys high-level cards far faster than one spread thin across all five gems.
- Read the board before taking tokens: let the cards you can see (and the ones you want to reserve) dictate which gems you collect, not the other way around.

## Tempo

- A turn that buys a card is almost always better than a turn that only takes tokens, since it adds a permanent bonus. Aim to buy on as many turns as possible.
- Reserve to smooth your tempo: it banks a card for later and gives a Gold that can cover a future shortfall, but it costs a turn, so reserve cards you genuinely plan to buy.
- Watch the token supply. In a 2-player game each gem has only 4 tokens, so taking 2 of a gem can starve an opponent who needs it.

## Nobles

- Nobles are 3 prestige for free, so shape your engine around the nobles in play. Two nobles sharing a colour pair is a strong signal to stack those colours.
- A noble needs 3 bonuses in each of three colours (or 4 in two colours). Counting your owned cards against the nobles row tells you how many buys away a visit is.
- Since nobles only count bonuses, a 0-prestige card that completes a noble is effectively worth 3 prestige - factor that in when choosing between cards.

## Gold and reserving

- Gold is the scarcest resource (5 total) and the only way to get it is reserving. Time your reserves so the Gold lands before you need it, not after.
- Reserving a card also hides it from opponents. If a high-value card on the board is one an opponent is clearly building toward, reserving it denies them even if you never buy it.
- Don't reserve speculatively with a full reserve - you forfeit the Gold and the tempo.

## The race to 15

- Reaching 15 prestige triggers the end but doesn't stop play immediately - everyone finishes the round, so opponents get equal turns to overtake you. Trigger the end only when your lead is safe, or when you're behind and need to cap the game.
- Because the final round is shared, a big single-turn jump (a high-prestige level 3 card plus a noble) can end the game before opponents can respond.
- When you're behind, accelerating toward 15 can be the right move even at lower prestige, since it limits how far ahead the leader can pull.

## Tiebreaks

- Ties are broken by number of development cards owned, and more cards wins. If the scores are close, buying extra cards (even cheap 0-prestige ones) is a legitimate way to win the tiebreak.
- Conversely, a lean high-prestige engine can lose a tie to a wider one - keep card count in mind when the finish is close.
