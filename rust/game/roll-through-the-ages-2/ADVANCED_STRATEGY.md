# Roll Through the Ages - Advanced Strategy

Higher-level strategic considerations.

## Building an engine

- Developments compound. Agriculture (+1 food per food die) plus Granaries (sell food for 6 coins) turns food dice into a coin engine; Masonry (+1 worker per worker die) plus Engineering (stone to 3 workers) turns worker dice into a building engine. Commit to a coherent engine rather than buying scattered developments.
- Coinage (coin dice worth 12 instead of 7) is far more valuable once you roll many dice. Growing your city first makes a coin engine much stronger.
- Preservation lets you double food for 1 pottery before rolling. Paired with Agriculture and Granaries it can feed a large city almost for free and bank surplus as coins.

## Goods as value and currency

- A good's value is triangular (n(n+1)/2 times its multiplier), so concentrated stacks are worth far more than spread-out singles. Three stone (value 12) beats one each of wood/stone/pottery (value 6). When you can choose, deepen a stack rather than spreading.
- Higher-multiplier goods (cloth x4, spearhead x5) are the most efficient per unit, but have the lowest caps. With Commerce (+1 point per good held), total goods count matters too, so balance depth against the 6-goods limit (or buy Caravans to remove it).
- Goods double as buying power. Holding a big stack lets you buy an expensive development (Architecture 60, Empire 70) in one shot by spending the whole stack - plan your purchases around the value you can accumulate.

## Monument racing

- The first player to complete a monument scores its higher value and everyone else scores the lower one. The gap is largest on the big monuments (Great Pyramid 12 vs 8, Wall 10 vs 6). Racing for first on a high-value monument is often worth concentrating workers.
- The Wall also blocks invasion disasters, so it is both a points race and a defensive buy - doubly valuable if opponents are rolling skulls.
- Remember the second end-game trigger: once all 7 monuments are built by someone, the final round starts. If you are behind on monuments, you may want to speed up or slow down monument completion to control when the game ends.

## Controlling the end game

- The final round triggers when any player reaches 7 developments OR all 7 monuments are built. Track both. If you are winning, you may want to trigger the end before opponents catch up; if you are behind, delay it.
- Empire (+1 point per city) and Architecture (+2 per monument) are expensive late developments that only pay off if the game lasts long enough. Buy them only when you can still benefit, or when you control the timing of the end.

## Defensive play

- Disaster points are subtracted directly and can sink an otherwise strong civilization. In a skull-heavy game, Irrigation (10) and Medicine (20) are cheap insurance; Smithing and Religion turn invasion and revolt into weapons against opponents.
- Smithing plus a stockpile of spearheads lets a 4-skull invasion hit every unprotected opponent and then invade for extra damage. If you pursue this, keep spearheads on hand and watch for opponents building the Wall.
- In multiplayer (3-4 players), disasters like pestilence hit everyone, so blocking them with Medicine gives you a relative edge over the whole table, not just one rival.

## Tempo

- Many phases skip automatically when you have no legal action, so a single `next` can cascade through several phases. Do not waste turns holding resources you cannot or will not spend - spend workers and coins the same turn you earn them when possible.
- Growing your city early increases every future roll (more dice) but raises your food bill immediately. The classic strong line is to grow the city a little, secure food production, then pivot into developments and monuments.
