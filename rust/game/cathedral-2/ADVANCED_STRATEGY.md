# Cathedral - Advanced Strategy

Higher-level strategic considerations.

## Territory control

- The board has 100 cells. The Cathedral takes 6, and both players' pieces total 47 cells each. Territory control determines who runs out of space first.
- Enclosing empty territory is as valuable as placing pieces. A region of 10 empty cells you claim is 10 cells your opponent cannot use.
- Use your pieces to create walls that divide the board. The player who controls more territory has more placement options in the endgame.

## Piece shape efficiency

- Compact pieces (squares, blocks) are easier to place than long, thin pieces. Play elongated pieces early while there is room.
- L-shaped and T-shaped pieces can wrap around corners, making them useful for enclosing regions.
- Single-cell pieces (size 1) are the most flexible. Save them for when nothing else fits.

## Cathedral positioning

- The Cathedral's placement defines the game's geography. A central Cathedral creates four quadrants; an edge Cathedral creates two zones.
- Place the Cathedral to split the board into regions where you have a positional advantage.
- The Cathedral can be captured (its territory flips), but it is never returned to hand. Its position is permanent even if ownership changes.

## Capture tactics

- Setting up a capture often requires multiple turns. Plan your enclosure over several placements.
- A region with two distinct piece identities cannot be captured. If your opponent has two different pieces in a region you are trying to enclose, you cannot capture it.
- Capturing the Cathedral's territory is possible and valuable: it flips the Cathedral's ownership to you, denying your opponent its blocking effect.

## Endgame

- The game ends when neither player can place anything. The player with fewer unplaced tiles wins.
- In the simultaneous end-phase (no open tiles remain), both players place independently. Speed of placement does not matter; only whether you can place at all.
- Calculate your remaining piece size versus your opponent's. If you are ahead, focus on placing pieces rather than capturing territory.
- If you are behind, capturing opponent pieces returns them to hand and increases their remaining piece size, closing the gap.

## Tempo

- Placing the Cathedral does not consume player 1's turn. Player 1 gets two placements before player 0's first turn. Use this initiative.
- If your opponent has no legal move, you keep taking turns. Creating a board state where your opponent is stuck gives you free placements.
- Do not rush to fill your own territory. Sometimes leaving gaps in your territory lets you place more pieces later.
